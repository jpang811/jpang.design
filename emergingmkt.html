Designing for Emerging Markets the Google Way

Understanding the everyday experiences I take for granted

What’s a Credit Card Anyway?


I’ve lived in developed markets all my life. Cell phones, credit cards, on-demand cabs and subway systems. They are my daily interactions.

In Emerging markets, however, users pay with cash whereas I use credit cards without thinking twice. They commute via motorbikes, and yet I own no vehicle and only commute by the subway. These users have motorbike insurances, and yet I don’t even have an insurance plan.

Designing for the Next Billion Users


Google has identified these emerging markets as the Next Billion Users who are going online via mobile and their challenge to stay connected whether it's by conserving data usage or providing better connectivity.

When I learned that Google was conducting a workshop on user research for these Next Billion Users, I signed up immediately. I wanted to improve my practice in inclusive design and for emerging markets.

My first experience of the Internet was ICQ and that dial-up connection.
— My Internet #ThrowBack

The Googlers kicked off the session with our first memory of the Internet. It was clear that our first times were very different from users in the emerging market, who likely went straight to mobile devices rather than desktop.

Designing for emerging markets requires an open mind and a much deeper and contextual understanding of their ways of life.

Immersive User Research


Just like ethnographic research, you have to immerse yourself in the user’s environment to understand their habits and what the artefacts at home symbolize.

Google’s design team embarked on an immersion trip to understand how motorbikers use Google Maps as a Go-Jek provider, the Indonesian motorcycle ride-hailing service.

The most interesting part is how they shared these insights with the wider team who weren’t able to experience it first-hand – through immersive stories. They recorded short 10-second videos and first-person photos to illustrate the motorbikers using Google Maps on the road.


Getting to Know the Users


Insightful user research requires planning.

Tasked to design a better experience for buyers and sellers, we were given the profile of Abagail, a talented entrepreneur and seller in Kenya, Africa. Based on her profile, we extracted keywords that represent her pain points, challenges and aspirations.

To develop a more in-depth understanding of her core needs, we also identified various research methods to achieve that:

Conversations to understand the current selling process online and offline at marketplaces.

Diary studies for sellers to record their operational pain points.

Shadow shopping both buyers and sellers at physical marketplaces to get a sense of their interactions and preferences.

Abagail's Core Needs


Abagail's core needs are much more than growing her business. Her primary motivation is to promote the Kenyan culture through her clothes and accessories.

To enable that, Abagail needs a mobile phone with a good camera, and stable connectivity to browse online for inspirations and promote on social media.


Design Principles Brain Dump


To better inform our final solution, our team did a brain dump of all the adjectives that respond to Abagail's needs. It was a hard exercise given that the closets synonyms make the subtlest difference.

As identified, her underyling motivation is her desire to promote and retain her Kenyan heritage. We settled on three key design principles - Connected, Communal and Easy.

The Guru Bar

What Abagail needed was not a prescription. Another app or a better camera isn't going to cut it. She needs guidance, a service solution to a sustainable expansion. Coincidentally, one of our ideation sessions had a "no-screen" constraint.

After several rounds of individual and group ideation, our team dot votted my initial idea of a dedicated seller's hotspot, which later morphed into the Guru Bar.

Think of the Guru Bar as the seller's Genius Bar where you have both the technology and human smarts (virtually in this case) to support seller needs in various ways.

From left to right, the hot spot provides a stable Internet connection for sellers to get on social media, browse for inspiration or simply set up shop.

The high-quailty camera is provided alongside a photoshoot station to optimize product shots.

Finally, to better empower sellers, the Guru Bar also provides guides on photoshooting and social media promotion best practices.

Ideally, this Guru Bar will be stationed at the marketplace as a communal way for seller peers to support and complement one another's business.

Learnings

I had forgotten how easy and accessible connectivity is until a recent client project and this workshop was a good reality check for me.

Being an empathetic designer is more than understanding user pain points, but also being mindful of the everyday interactions we in developed markets experience are often taken for granted.

As the only designer in the team, I've always had to do quick and dirty user researches. This workshop has helped tremendously in honing my craft. and I'm excited to have adopted this practice in my design toolkit.
