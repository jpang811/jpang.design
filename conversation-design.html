Making Conversations Human

Conversation Component Design

How Does One Sound 'Human'?


Designing for conversations all come down to the micro interactions.

Every word, phrase or sentence can influence the conversation flow, evoke emotions, and increase engagements or cause drop-outs as a result. Words are the only interface and it all comes down to the details – words.

I was working on a chatbot project that was hypothesizing conversational experiences as a means of content discovery instead of browsing a website, as part of an experimental project at a bank’s innovation lab.

Conversation Component


I joined the project mid-way and was tasked to design for enhancements, the little details – you know, the words.

As digital interfaces are composed of UI components, conversations are composed of conversation components.

Depending on the platform, the conversation components vary. Comparing Facebook Messenger and Slack components in their closest forms:

Facebook 'Quick Replies' vs Slack 'Interactive Message Buttons'


Facebook 'Templates' vs Slack 'Actions'


For more information on conversation components, have a look at their documentation and I also highly recommend this book to Designing Chatbots.

For my project, the platform was Messenger.

Conversation Interactions to Enhance


Tasked with the following challenges, I took a top-down approach by first studying the current conversation flow and then zooming in on each challenge's micro-interactions.

User Consent Agreement

Given the importance of data privacy and security, user consent is no doubt required but generally considered the least favorite part of the experience.

To make this slightly more engaging, I looked at how it’s currently done.

On Messenger, there are two parts to the experience - the welcome screen, with a 1000 character limit and then the chatbot’s introduction.


Facebook Messenger's welcome screen example

Given these two component options, I could insert the user consent agreement in either the welcome screen or at the introduction.

I mocked up these two versions and validated it with a small sample of users:

Version 1: getting user consent in the welcome screen


Version 2: getting user consent in the introduction screen

The final verdict was version 2. Having the user consent agreement on the welcome screen makes the experience feel less clunky and more up front.



Change Language

In a near bi-lingual market like Hong Kong, we need to design a functionality to change languages. In most digital experiences, this function is nested inside the menu or settings. Adhering to this wired behaviour, this is the first place we will have it.

{ Placeholder image }

Hong Kong is relatively bi-lingual but Cantonese (a dialect of Chinese) is still the primary language over English. As a result, global brands like Google has defaulted our online experiences to Cantonese.

For example, my Google account preferences are all set in English. But whenever I open a Google Maps link or connect with my Google account for logins, the default language is Cantonese.

Luckily, I am a bi-lingual. But for English-speakers, how would they know what the change language option is if it’s in a foreign language?


An example of connecting with Google Login where the default language is Cantonese for Hong Kong

The first touch I introduced was making the change language option bi-lingual.

The most common path to the need of changing languages is usually at the beginning of the conversation. The current component is already nested inside the menu, so I thought how can we bring this experience forward and make it just a little more delightful by making use of Messenger’s ‘Quick Reply’ component.

I prototyped the conversation by speaking out loud and decided to test the sequence of which this language change function is best introduced without being too long-winded and not confusing.


An example of connecting with Google Login where the default language is Cantonese for Hong Kong

Validate between: - Quick reply component after introduction - After full intro and T&Cs Additional feature: - In menu, make menu items bi-lingual as well o Usually people can guess how to navigate to menu
