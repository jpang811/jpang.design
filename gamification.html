Building Sustainable Engagement

After pioneering the Personalization and Engagement modules as part of the Data-Driven UX Design Series, I knew the nuts and bolts to designing and applying them in business. But something still felt missing - it's not sustainable.

Life is a Game

At the time of this realization, Pokemon Go had just launched and I had picked up FitBit again. Gamification

It was such an obvious choice - Gamification creates Sustainable Engagement.

I spent the next few weeks reading all about gamification and enrolled in an online course from Coursera by UPenn's Professor Kevin Werbach. (Great course, by the way)

To begin, one must understand the crucial difference between pure Games vs Game Design.

"Gamification is the use of game elements and game design techniques in non-game contexts."


- Professor Kevin Warbach

The Framework

In one of my projects #TravelGoals, I modeled after Professor Warbach's framework.

Motivators

Motivation drives behavior and is the heart of gamification.

There are 3 classifications of motivators and each is more effective than others when activated at specific stages of the customer journey or for certain industries.

Positive Motivators allow users to craft their own strategy within the game and give users a sense of meaning and progress.

Neutral Motivators are about the social interactions and badge collection mechanisms alike.


Negative Motivators instills a sense of urgency and scarcity to prompt the user to take action otherwise.

Habit-Forming

The key to loyalty is getting users Hooked, borrowing the term from author Nir Eyal. Gamification builds on the same concept of building Habits in the form of loops.


Engagement Loop is about keeping the user hooked with the right incentives.

Progression Loop is very much like designing user journeys, taking them through one stage after another.

Game Design

Game Design is the orchestration and engineering of game elements and transforming them into great user experiences or in this context, a fun game. As with my FitBit, it's the orchestration of game elements like Badges, Leaderboard, Levels and Social Challenges that bring about the fun in fitness.

Starting with #TravelGoals

#TravelGoals is about helping users attain their travel goal by keeping track of their progress, recommendations to fast track and finally celebrating that special moment.

[userflow]

As a first step, users pick their travel goal and depending on their pick, the user will then land on one of four travel guides corresponding to the "vibe" of the destination.

The travel guides are designed to inspire and convey the destination‚Äôs vibes and what the user can expect. The goal is to engage the user and guide them along the way with relevant recommendations and offers.

The Real Deal

The mechanism to achieving the travel goal is based on the users miles accrual. Based on users‚Äô miles balance and speed of accumulation, I worked with Data Scientists to cluster them by using a magic IF formula to sort users into different buckets based on those criteria, a concept introduced in the Personalization module.

[placeholder sketch]

Based on each user's current state and travel goal, we followed-up with a relevant offer and personalized communication.

We captured 40+% of the audience and contributed over 3,000 unique views to the travel guides.

Gamification as a Holistic Strategy

#TravelGoals proved to be a great test case for Gamification but it's just the beginning!

After using this framework, I realized how easily it can be applied to different businesses and user experiences. For loyalty programs as a start, its nature of points, miles and rewards make a sound basis for gamification. Another common use case is for people management and talent development by incentivizing users to complete "challenges" and unlocking rewards as they progress through them.

Married with the principles of consumer psychology,... Gamification makes a great addition to the field of User Experience Design.
