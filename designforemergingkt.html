Designing for Emerging Markets, the Google Way

Understanding the experiences I take for granted

What’s a Credit Card Anyway?


I’ve lived in developed markets all my life. Cell phones, credit cards, on-demand cabs and subway systems. They are my daily interactions.

In emerging markets, users settle payments with cash whereas I use credit cards without thinking twice. They commute via motorbikes, and yet I own no vehicle and only commute by the subway. These users have motorbike insurances, and yet I don’t even have an insurance plan.

Designing for the Next Billion Users


Google has identified these emerging markets as the Next Billion Users who are going online via mobile and how to help these users stay connected whether it's by conserving data usage or providing better connectivity.

When I learned that Google was conducting a workshop on user research for these Next Billion Users, I signed up immediately. I wanted to improve my practice in inclusive design for emerging markets.

My first experience of the Internet was ICQ and that dial-up connection.
— My Internet #ThrowBack

The Googlers kicked off our session with our first memory of the Internet. It was clear that our first times are very different from emerging market users, who likely went straight to mobile devices rather than desktop.

Designing for emerging markets requires an open mind and a much deeper and contextual understanding of their ways of life.


Immersive User Research

Just like you would with ethnographic research, you have to immerse yourself in the user’s environment, habits and by looking at their artefacts at home.

When Google Maps was designing for motorbikers in Indonesia, their design team physically immersed themselves in Indonesia to understand their context of navigation and physical interactions such as how they place their phones on the dashboard or whether they preferred voice command for directions.

In order for other team members or developers who weren't there to experience it first-hand, they captured immersive short stories. They recorded 10-second videos and first-person photos to illustrate the motorbiker’s current interactions using Google Maps.


Getting to Know the User


Insightful user research requires planning.

Tasked to design a better experience for buyers and sellers, we were given the profile of Abagail, a talented entrepreneur and seller in Kenya, Africa.

We extracted the keywords that represented her pain points, challenges and aspirations. To develop a more in-depth understanding of her core needs, we identified various research methods to achieve that.


Conversations

To understand current process to selling goods off and online.

Diary Study

Document the process and pain points of being a seller.

Shadow Research

Shadowing both buyers and sellers at marketplaces.

Defining Core Needs

Once we understand the dynamics and relationships between buyer and sellers, pain and delight points. We set out to define their core needs.


Design Principles Brain Dump


With needs defined, we set out to define the design principles, an often underlooked living document in my opinion. Our team just did a brain dump of all the adjectives that respond to Abagail's needs and it was a hard exercise. Even the closest synonym make the subtlest difference, something I learned during the inception phase of defining products in my design work.

What stood out to us just by reading Abagail's profile was the fact that her underyling motivation to making a living is her desire to promote and retain her Kenyan heritage. Because of that, we settled on the three design principles - Connected, Communal and Easy.

Introducing Kollabo

Kollabo is a chatbot platform for collaborative group destination discovery and trip booking. The idea is to simplify the planning process by having Kollabo facilitate the conversation, offload the Designated Booker's responsibility and help the Uncommitted commit to travel plans. It's about making group travel easy.

In order to provide value-add, we introduced an automated flight booking function by integrating the API solutions provided by the sponsors.
