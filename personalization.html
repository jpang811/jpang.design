Personalizing Your Experience


Also published on Medium.com at

Grounded to the Roots of Hospitality

My background and education is highly rooted in hospitality and the basics is addressing the guest by name. But what makes personalization so special?

It’s that fuzzy feeling of comfort and familiarity when the barista remembers that your favourite drink is soy mocha and paired with a multigrain bagel and low-fat cream cheese.

Personalization comes in many forms can be applied in so many industries as I have in my previous work experiences in retail and travel.

The problem today is that it's less commonly acknowledged never mind practiced, especially for online experiences.

What is Personalization?

Personalization is beyond retargeting. Just because I searched for a dress on Nordstrom, then I am algorithmically qualified for a series of near-intrusive advertisements asking me over and over if I was interested in making the purchase.

For indecisive customers like myself, online shopping can play down impulsive purchases but if I keep thinking about the dress for a few days then I will most likely to purchase it. Then in that case, regardless of retargeted ads, I was likely to take that action.

I would argue that current efforts in ad retargeting is not half the story of what personalization is. In fact, when designing personalization, it should be part of the overall strategy under user engagement and not seen as one of the features in a campaign.

Three Types of Personalization Features

Basics

The bare minimum of personalization is addressing the user by name or basic demographic features. "Miss Pang, welcome back. Miss Pang, how is life in Hong Kong?". I can almost hear Siri’s warm, friendly tone.


Behavior-Based

Behaviour is very particular to your business and industry, typically measured in transactions and milestones. In the context of my case study in travel, it is mostly driven by travel frequency, destination and transactional behaviour at particular touch points of the travel journey.

Engagement-Driven

Engagement-driven are personalization features which you introduce and become new data points in your data bank as a result.

I’ve shed some light on Part 2 of this Data-Driven UX Design series where we launched an engagement survey to discover users' travel personas. The idea is if your member or customer answers to your survey and you respond to that, it becomes a personalization feature you can utilize.

Personalization is all about targeting the Right User, at the Right Time with the Right Offer.

That's Personalization Done Right

The power of personalization lies in using the features in the right context. In some cases, one personalization feature may resonate more with one user than the other, at one time over another. But how is it determined? That’s where machine learning algorithms come into play - to rank their importance and then assign these features to members.

In a travel campaign I've done in the past, If you traveled with us the past year, it is our window to entice you with another travel hence #WhereNext. We’d like to gently remind you of the time at a specific destination and your milestones with us.

If you have flown over thousands of miles, you are likely to receive the “x miles flown with us” feature. Alternatively, if you are new to the program and have not accumulated enough transactions, you will like lye receive the “x miles balance”.

Personalization is a really nice-to-have and often times unexpected feature. In our performance, we acheived 30% open and 4% click.

The Complexity of Personalization

In the case of the #TraveMilestones above, it was easy because I simply used IF statements to make it dynamic content. But what if the personalization feature was much more ingrained with content? For example:

We launched an engagement survey for members to discover their travel personas - Captain, Chief, Executive or Adventure Seeker. Just from the names, you can already tell that each persona has distinct personalities and behavior, so that means the content you write for each persona must be dynamically written for the persona. In fact, I had to develop multiple copies of an email just to cater to each persona.

When writing to the Captain, I would use language like “We know time is of essence when it comes to traveling and we know just the right offer for you." Whereas if I was addressing an Adventure Seeker, I would say “We know you love a great adventure and looks like we have just the right offer for a #Globetrotter like yourself!” You can see difference in tones and design which convey assertiveness for the Captain versus playfulness for the Adventure Seeker.
