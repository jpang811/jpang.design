User Engagement is a Conversation

Part 2 of 3 Data-Driven UX Series

There's more to Personalization

As with Part 1 of the Data-Driven UX Series, personalization is a great conversation starter. In order to continue the conversation, I need to engage users and hopefully influence their behavior.

One of the problem statemenets I tackled was user acquisition, specifically credit cards for this case study. I always begin with understanding users. Some prefer facts and figures, pros and cons, whereas some are driven by emotions. Striking that delicate balance between both groups can be challenging.

What's the Deal with Credit Cards?

Credit card campaigns can get very old nowadays. Typically, you receive either a Direct Mail or email with a cookie-cutter message that says "Apply for our credit card and get offer XYZ. Limited Time Offer!". Let's be real, when is it not Limited Time Offer?

The most logical approach is to use machine learning algorithms alike to predict users' likelihood to apply for a credit card. But this doesn't take into account users' emotions and motivations.

To understand members from both qualitative and quantitative aspects, I decided to introduce an two-part Engagement piece - an Engagement Email which lands on a Buzzfeed-inspired Engagement Survey.

User Journey Flow from the Engagement email, to Engagement survey, to Acquisition email

Designing the Engagement

There are two challenges to this piece. First, how do I get users to click on the Engagement Email? Second, once they land on the Engagement Survey, how do I get them to complete it?

The idea sparked when I was on Flipboard and came across this pop-up.

So this is what I did.

We A/B tested the subject title and travel quotes and the open rates skyrocketed to over 40% and over 10% for click rates.

Whether you agree or disagree, brace yourself for a just-for-fun Engagement Survey.

So Tell Me, What's Your Travel Persona?

The trick to designing the survey is it isn't just about fun, viral content. It should serve a purpose.

In addition to engagement, I did a quick analysis on the offers' competitive advantages which I used them as anchor points to gauge users' interest towards the offer.

Most of us, it seems, are Adventure Seekers at heart.

We hit a good 10% completion rate which were quite impressive given there was no offer attached.

Of course, great things build expectations. So after kicking off with a successful engagement piece, my fellow data scientists got cracking with the survey data and personas so we could follow-up with an acquisition email a week later.

Acquisition Email

This is where the magic happens. Each persona was pre-assigned to a set of personalized content which we can map to corresponding personas.

For those who had not completed out the survey, we assigned them to a travel persona based on characteristics such as demographics and transactional behavior.

Acquisition Emails personalized for each persona

The result? A whopping 35%+ open and 4% click rates.

Closing the Loop

As a final wrap-up to this campaign, we launched a reminder email another week later based on users' email behavior. I personalized the message slightly for three states: Open Only, Click Only and Clicked and Applied. As you may have guessed, the Click Only registered more interest and conversion than Open Only.

What I Learned

Engagement costs nothing.

Turns out that users are willing to engage even with no incentives. Another equally rewarding learning is the ability to influence user behavior - low propensity yet high engaged users. The only expectation, though, is that you follow-up.

This is the heart of data-driven design and could not have been done without either the engagement nor the quantitative propensity model. In fact, this approach maximized our reach and acquisition numbers.
