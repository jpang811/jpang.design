Designing for Emerging Markets the Google Way

Understanding the experiences I take for granted

What’s a Credit Card Anyway?


I’ve lived in developed markets all my life. Cell phones, credit cards, on-demand cabs and subways. They are my daily interactions.

In emerging markets, users pay with cash whereas I use credit cards without thinking twice. They commute via motorbikes, yet I own no vehicle and only commute by the subway. These users have motorbike insurances, yet I don’t even have an insurance plan.


Designing for the Next Billion Users


Google has identified these emerging markets as the Next Billion Users who are going online via mobile and an opportunity to help these users stay connected whether it's by conserving data usage or providing better connectivity.

When I learned that Google was conducting a workshop on user research for these Next Billion Users, I signed up immediately. I wanted to improve my practice in inclusive design for emerging markets.

My first experience of the Internet was ICQ and that dial-up connection.
— My Internet #ThrowBack

The Googlers kicked off the session with each participant's first memory of the Internet. Not surprisingly, our first times were very different from users in the emerging market, who likely went straight to mobile devices rather than desktop.

Designing for emerging markets requires an open mind and a deeper contextual understanding of their ways of life.

Immersive User Research


Just like ethnographic research, you have to immerse yourself in the user’s environment in order to understand their habits and what the artefacts at home symbolize.

For Google Maps in Indonesia, Google’s design team embarked on an immersion trip to understand how motorbikers use Google Maps as a Go-Jek provider, the Indonesian motorcycle ride-hailing service.

The most interesting part of their approach is how they shared these insights with the wider team who weren’t able to experience it first-hand – through immersive stories. They recorded short 10-second videos and first-person photos to illustrate the motorbikers using Google Maps on the road.


Getting to Know the User


Insightful user research requires planning.

Tasked to design a better experience for buyers and sellers, we were given the profile of Abagail, a talented entrepreneur and seller in Kenya, Africa.

We extracted keywords that represented her pain points, challenges and aspirations. To develop a more in-depth understanding of her core needs, we identified various research methods to achieve that.

Conversations

To understand current process to selling goods off and online.

Diary Study

Document the process and pain points of being a seller.

Shadow Research

Shadowing both buyers and sellers at marketplaces.

Abagail's Core Needs


Abagail's core needs are primarily motivated by her want to promote the Kenyan culture through her clothes and accessories.

To enable that, she needs a phone with a good camera, and stable connectivity to browse for inspirations online and promote on social media.

Design Principles Brain Dump


With needs defined, we set out to define the design principles, an often underlooked living document in my opinion. Our team just did a brain dump of all the adjectives that respond to Abagail's needs and it was a hard exercise.

Even the closest synonym make the subtlest difference, something I learned during the inception phase of defining products in my design work.


We settled on Connected, Communal and Easy, which corresponds to Abagail's technological needs, motivations and desired experience respectively.

The Guru Bar

What Abagail needed was not a prescription. We wanted to enable her to grow and retain her community values. Another app or a better camera isn't gonna cut it nor does it respond to her deeper motivation. Coincidentally, one of our ideation sessions had a "no-screen" constraint.

After several rounds of individual and group ideation, our team dot votted on my initial idea of a dedicated hotspot for sellers like Abagail which eventually became the Guru Bar

She needed empowerment to ensure stable connectivity for her cell phone usage, a communal way to increase her reach by providing a supportive environment and seller peers, and finally making it easy for her to reach her goals with the appropriate resources (tips on product photoshoots and social media)

Learnings


I had forgotten how easy and accessible connectivity is until I’ve started designing for emerging markets at this workshop and for client projects. Being an empathetic designer is more than just understanding users' pain points, but the ability to relate to the everyday experiences many of us in developed markets take for granted.

As a lone designer in the team, I’ve always had to do quick and dirty user researches and this workshop has helped tremendously in honing my craft.

Special thanks to Raj, Lauren and Chandrika for hosting the workshop.
